## When do I need a transfer mechanism like SCC's? {#slide-5}

5

There are several mechanism available under the EU GDPR, one is concluding SCC's.  If the vendor is located in the US, another mechanism is the vendor being certified under the EU/US privacy framework.  You can check a vendor's status  here . 

SCC's are EU-approved contract terms that protect data when it\'s sent to a country outside the EEA that doesn't have "adequate" data protection laws.  It is a standard document that does not allow any changes.

All of the information 

The SCC's will be applicable even when there is no physical transfer, but remote access of data.

Scenario 1:   You would need a  transfer mechanism  when you are planning on transferring data from the EU/EEA to a non-adequate country like the USA, China ( physical transfer  out of EU/EEA or  remote access  to data in the EU/EEA).  

Example:  AZ provides remote access to a clinical data set within AZ systems, under appropriate restrictions and access controls or AZ sent the data directly to a vendor in the US via secure channels.

Scenario 2:  You would also need a transfer mechanism if AZ AB is acting as a controller and instructing a vendor (processor) located in the USA to collect and process data on AZ\'s behalf and sent it back to AZ ( i.e. no physical transfer out of the EU --  see slide 6 ).

 

Example:  AZ AB is instructing a vendor to collect and process  coded  data in a clinical study in Canada and then sent this data back to AZ AB. We are therefore instructing the vendor in Canada to "act in AZ shoes" so to speak.

## Standard Contractual Clauses (SCC's) as a transfer mechanism in cases where AZ instructs a vendor to transfer data from outside the EEA: {#slide-6}

6

You would need SCC's when there is a transfer of data  from  the EEA/UK  to  a non-adequate country (i.e. USA, China, Brazil etc.)

However, in certain instances SCC's might be necessary where AZ will be  receiving  the data (i.e. there is no physical transfer of data out of the EU). This can be tricky and confusing to understand. 

Where an EU/EEA based controller/exporter  (AZ) instructs a vendor outside the EEA to process personal data for AZ purposes , the GDPR treats this as  an international transfer  -- even if the data never physically leaves the country where it was collected.  

Under relevant EU Guidelines, this means that the exporter "transmits or otherwise makes available the data" to another controller or processor located in another country and therefore the EU GDPR will apply.

The trigger is where the  controller is located  (EEA/UK).

What does this mean in practice?

Even if AZ is not physically sending data out of the EEA/UK to a vendor but rather is instructing a vendor outside of the EEA to collect or process personal data for AZ's purposes, the GDPR treats this as AZ  exporting  the processing activity which would trigger the application of Chapter V of the EU GDPR.

This means a transfer "tool" is therefore required -- usually the SCC's.

AZ will be the "exporter" and the vendor will be the "importer" for purposes of completing the SCC's.

## Completing SCC's: {#slide-7}

7

SCC's is a standard document issued by the  EU commission  .

The SCC text is non-negotiable and cannot be changed .  You can only elect certain choices ( i.e  number of days for a certain action or member state governing law).

Annex 1 (A. List of Parties):  must be completed in full must be properly signed by all the parties to be legally effective. 

Annex I (B. Description of transfer):  must be completed with all the relevant details in full (categories of data subjects; categories of personal data; sensitive categories of data; frequency of transfer; nature of processing; purpose of the data transfer; and period of transfer.

Annex I (C. Competent Supervisory authority):  can be left unchanged if using AZ template.

Annex II -- Technical and Organisational Measures (TOM's):  please ensure to conduct the relevant  IT Security Assessment  to facilitate a data transfer.  The security assessment outcome will form your TOM's and it can be copied from the outcome provided by cybersecurity.  Please do not delete this appendix  -- it is legally required and can make the document non-binding if not included.

Exhibit Y (Supplementing SCC's):   only include if your TIA indicates that you should.

UK SCC's:  only include if UK data is in scope.

Swiss SCC's:  only include if Swiss data is in scope.
